Hannut - GOSS 1

Dec 23 2022

Finally, time to get my first Grand Operational Simulation Series (GOSS) game on the table. This game started Dec. 23, 2022, with the playthrough posted at the end of the game.

Hannut is a "GOSS-Lite" having arrived as part of World At War magazine #80. The full GOSS rules (2020 edition) are around 80 pages. Hannut rules are 16 pages.

Since I sorted all the counters by unit when I clipped the corners, set up was easy. Even easier as I have no idea what I'm doing, so I just spread the counters on the map and will see how the game plays out. It's going to be pretty ugly but that's ok.

Turn 1

Command Phase (AM GT only)

  1. The supply status of all their HQs and units. I'm going to assume everything is in supply at the start of the game.

  2. The night activity markers they will place in the Night GT Box for that GD. None for the first turn, make it easy.

  3. The movement status for their sideâ€™s HQs. Mobile for both HQ.

  4. The receipt of air points (AP). Added markers to track on the map.

  5. The receipt and assignment of barrage points (BP) to HQs. Barrage points:

    • 4th Pz: 9 + 0 Corps BP
    • 2d DM: 7 + 2 Corps BP
    • 3d DM: 7 + 5 Corps BP

German Player Turn

Active Player Movement Phase

1) (French phasing player only) The French player may flip any IP under construction markers so that their IP completed side shows.

2) The active player may place or remove the below markers on any eligible friendly units. If a unit is marked with an Exp, CR, or IP under construction marker, that unit cannot move during this Movement Phase.

  • Exploitation (Exp)
  • Prepared Assault (PA): Units marked with PA marker may move.
  • Combat Reserve (CR)
  • Improved position (IP): Under Construction (French only)

3) Place reinforcements due to arrive during the GT. They may move in Step 3.

4) Move eligible active units in any order.

(Special German Exploitation Phase): The German player may move any friendly units marked with an Exp marker up to one-half of their movement allowance. They may conduct overrun combat (13.13).

Here's what we have after the Germans make their move:

Active Player Combat Phase:

1) The active player places tactical assault (TA) markers on top of friendly units that will attack without using PA.

Added 3 to the Germans.

2) The active player conducts Air FS missions.

Germans will not commit any Air this attack.

3) The non-active player conducts artillery, then Air FS missions.

The French do not respond this attack.

4) The active player conducts artillery FS missions.

Not this turn.

5) The active player may remove attack markers (both TA and PA) as necessary or desired.

Keeping all of them on since no fire support (FS) missions conducted.

6) The active player conducts all TA and PA ground assaults. After completion of all GAs, remove all AS markers.

And off we go.

Ground Assault (GA) 1

Many hours of reading rules for both Hannut and the full GOSS rules set. I'm going to end up printing out some player aids for sure.

The following combat rounds are tracked on a Google Sheet. An Excel file can be made available.

Combat 1: 5707 to 5607, result is German retreat, the way I read the rules is that I need to make a maximum retreat unless making and passing a proficiency check. Since this is a mech stack, retreat 1 hexes as there is only 1 discretionary hit.

Combat 2: 5705 to 5605, result is attacker with 1 discretionary hit, defender takes 2 discretionary hits. The German attacker passes a PRC and takes a step loss on the lead unit 1./7 Auf. The defender chooses to make PRC and fails with a valid retreat path. Since the artillery unit isn't even supposed to be in this game with these rules, going to eliminate it.

Combat 3: 5703 to 5604, the result is a German 1(2), which is terrible for the Germans, but no big deal, learning games are usually laughably bad. Since the II/35 was the lead unit, it will take the step loss, the German PRC passed so the stack retreats to 5903. The French got a * result and passed PRC yielding no effect.

And that's enough for this weekend. The game is moved to the back room where I'll play it out over the next couple of months.

What to play next?

Deciding what to play next is always a tough call when faced with a closet full of games, all of which were purchased with intent to play. Here's one I'm dealing with the decision.

  • Something New: this means both something new to me and a game which has been published recently, within the last two or three years.
  • Something Classic: I've acquired a number of games properly considered "classic" which haven't yet been played. The current example would be PanzerGruppe Guderian.
  • Something Again: mastery requires constant practice, which means playing games many, many times to allow nuance to appear.
  • Something Vietnam: as I'm on a Vietnam binge. I don't have all the Vietnam games ever published, but I can say that I have a lot of them, and probably more than most people.
  • Something Obscure: as it's fun to poke around in the dustier corners of game design.
  • Something Different: break away from the same old publishers and try a game from one of the new kids on the block. Or, conversely, dip into legacy with a classic tried and true.
  • Something Light: once in a while it's nice to actually finish a game all the way through in one sitting, in an hour or two.
  • Something Heavy: if possible, it's nice to set up something big and complicated which can be left out for days or weeks or even months, for a leisurely stroll through a very large or long game.

There are surely many more ways to choose, but the above method keeps me busy enough, helps me get both breadth and depth into my play, and provides me with a sense of progress.

The odd man out

When there is an odd man out:

  • Have a solo game ready to play.
  • Keep score or advise on a game in progress.
  • Referee a game in progress.
