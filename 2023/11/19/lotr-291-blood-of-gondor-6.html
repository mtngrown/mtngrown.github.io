Taking Basic Outlands 3.0 up against Blood of Gondor again. We'll see how it works out. I like this quest so far, and it may go into rotation.

Update: I forgot to add The Cross-Roads to the staging area, rendering this play moot. Since that is a Siege quest while it's in play, there are two options: 1. get it out of play as fast as possible, or leave it in and focus on Siege questing through Stage 1. The Threat value is only 1 for 1 player, so it's potentially an avenue of approach. Might do some deckbuilding to deal with it instead, replace the Spirit cards with Tactics cards.

AAR

Update: this is an invalid play: I did not put The Cross-Roads into the Staging Area at the start of the game, which renders the whole game moot as it requires Siege questing. I was wondering why this felt so much easier than previous plays!

I know Outlands is pooh-pooh'ed by the community, but I've found it's not been the easiest deck for me to pilot, and piloting it well requires getting through the first couple of turns. From previous experience, if I can't get the deck firing by Turn 4, it's probably going to fail.

For Blood of Gondor, I think the key is dealing with Hidden Cards right away. Don't let them build up. Remember that Faramir is going to do 3 damage where most of the enemies are 3 hits. The Brutal Uruk's are 5 hits, but only 2 defense, so if Faramir can put 3 hits on one, it's not too difficult to knock them out same turn. Also, there are a fair number of Treacheries and Locations in the deck, all of which whiff when exposed as Hidden cards.

What to play next?

Deciding what to play next is always a tough call when faced with a closet full of games, all of which were purchased with intent to play. Here's one I'm dealing with the decision.

  • Something New: this means both something new to me and a game which has been published recently, within the last two or three years.
  • Something Classic: I've acquired a number of games properly considered "classic" which haven't yet been played. The current example would be PanzerGruppe Guderian.
  • Something Again: mastery requires constant practice, which means playing games many, many times to allow nuance to appear.
  • Something Vietnam: as I'm on a Vietnam binge. I don't have all the Vietnam games ever published, but I can say that I have a lot of them, and probably more than most people.
  • Something Obscure: as it's fun to poke around in the dustier corners of game design.
  • Something Different: break away from the same old publishers and try a game from one of the new kids on the block. Or, conversely, dip into legacy with a classic tried and true.
  • Something Light: once in a while it's nice to actually finish a game all the way through in one sitting, in an hour or two.
  • Something Heavy: if possible, it's nice to set up something big and complicated which can be left out for days or weeks or even months, for a leisurely stroll through a very large or long game.

There are surely many more ways to choose, but the above method keeps me busy enough, helps me get both breadth and depth into my play, and provides me with a sense of progress.
